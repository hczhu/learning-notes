"Sleep Chart of biological ageing clocks in middle and late life." *Nature* (2026), published 13 May 2026. Open access. DOI: [10.1038/s41586-026-10524-5](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10524-5)
- **One-liner**: Across 9 brain/body systems and 3 omics layers, self-reported sleep duration has a **U-shaped** relationship with biological ageing — the youngest biological age is reached at ~6.4–7.8 h of sleep, and both short (<6 h) and long (>8 h) sleep track with faster ageing, higher systemic disease risk, and greater mortality.

- ## Core Concept: "Sleep Chart"
	- A framework that maps **self-reported sleep duration** against **23 biological ageing clocks** to quantify how sleep relates to organ- and system-level biological age.
	- Analogy: like a pediatric growth chart, but for sleep vs. biological ageing — it identifies where on the sleep spectrum biological age gaps are minimized.
	- Central readout is the **biological age gap**: (predicted biological age) − (chronological age). A positive gap = "older than your years" for that organ/omic; the goal is to find the sleep duration that minimizes this gap.

- ## The 23 Biological Ageing Clocks
	- Derived from three complementary data modalities:
		- **In vivo imaging** — clocks for 9 distinct brain and body systems (organ-specific biological ages).
		- **Plasma proteomics** — protein-based ageing clock(s).
		- **Plasma metabolomics** — metabolite-based ageing clock(s).
	- This multi-organ, multi-omics design is the key differentiator: rather than a single whole-body age, it resolves ageing **system by system**, revealing organ-specific sleep sensitivity.

- ## Dataset
	- **UK Biobank**, participants aged **37–84 years** (middle and late life).
	- Sleep exposure is **self-reported duration** (not objective actigraphy) — an important caveat for interpretation.

- ## Key Findings
	- ### 1. Systemic U-shaped pattern
		- A consistent U-shape emerges between sleep duration and biological age gaps across **all nine brain/body systems and all three omics technologies** — i.e., it is systemic, not confined to the brain.
		- **Optimal sleep** (lowest biological age gap) sits between **6.4 and 7.8 hours**, varying by **organ and sex**.
	- ### 2. Short and long sleep raise disease and mortality risk
		- Relative to **normal sleep (6–8 h)**, both **short (<6 h)** and **long (>8 h)** sleep are associated with:
			- Increased risk of **systemic diseases beyond the brain** (e.g., depression, diabetes).
			- Increased **all-cause mortality**.
		- Evidence triangulated from **genetic correlations** and **time-to-incident survival predictions**.
	- ### 3. Divergent pathways to late-life depression
		- The mechanisms linking abnormal sleep to **late-life depression differ by direction**:
			- **Long sleep** → association is **partially mediated** by ageing clocks (biological ageing sits on the causal path).
			- **Short sleep** → shows a **more direct link**, less mediated by the ageing clocks.
	- ### 4. Causality caveat (Mendelian randomization)
		- MR does **not** provide strong evidence that **disease causally affects sleep** (reverse causation), but it **cannot fully exclude** reverse causality either.
		- So the U-shape is best read as a robust cross-organ association with suggestive, not definitive, directionality.

- ## Takeaways
	- Sleep's relationship to ageing is **cross-organ and multi-omics**, not brain-specific — a whole-body phenomenon.
	- There is a **quantifiable sweet spot** (~6.4–7.8 h) that shifts by organ and sex, supporting personalized rather than one-size-fits-all sleep targets.
	- **Sleep optimization** is framed as a lever to promote healthy ageing, lower disease risk, and extend longevity.
	- Limitations to keep in mind: sleep is **self-reported**, the design is largely **cross-sectional/observational**, and causal direction remains only partially resolved.
