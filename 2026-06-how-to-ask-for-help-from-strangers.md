- **One-liner**: Asking for help is a learnable skill, not a personality trait — and the whole skill reduces to one principle: **put the other person's mind first, not your own request.**

- ## The One Principle
	- **Put yourself in their mind.** All good communication is grounded in understanding the reader's mind. Most people get help-asking wrong because they put *themselves* (and their project) at the front of the request instead of the other person.
	- Everything below is just **heuristics** serving this principle — you can reorder or drop them, as long as you're thinking from the reader's perspective.

- ## Heuristic 1 — Help is about people before projects
	- Someone helping your *project* is predicated on them wanting to help *you*. So show you're **worth helping** — i.e. show you're a **serious person**. Three sources of credibility, strongest to weakest:
		- **Proof of work (strongest)**: don't just *claim* you want to enter ML or learn to lift — show a trained model, a blog post with depth and thought, a vlog of your training. Actions, not aspirations.
		- **Personal connection**: "Steve suggested I reach out" situates you warmly. But it **borrows against someone else's credibility** — risky if they dislike Steve, or if you underdeliver relative to Steve's billing (which then hurts Steve too).
		- **Institutional credibility (weakest)**: naming a famous university/employer. At best it proves you cleared a filter *once* and nothing more; it doesn't situate you to *them* specifically and can read as status-signalling. Use sparingly, never as your only source.

- ## Heuristic 2 — Explain context, briefly
	- Before the ask, answer "what is going on here?" If Heuristic 1 worked, you've **borrowed their attention** — the most precious currency — so spend it judiciously.
	- Your description must be **so short as to be unsummarizable**. Connect your context to what *they already know*:
		- To an elected representative: skip your club's internal factionalism; explain how the club connects to *their legislative priorities*.
		- To a scientist you want an internship from: skip "I've loved science since childhood"; say you *implemented and extended their 2023 paper*.

- ## Heuristic 3 — Make the request easy to accept (reduce the cost of acceptance)
	- **Magnitude**: ask for 20 minutes, not "read my 500-page manuscript in a week."
	- **Specific**: "a resource to start with" beats "can I pick your brain?"
	- **Low friction**: asking for an intro? Write a forwardable blurb about yourself. Have a question? Ask it in writing, not over a call.
	- **Bounded**: don't ask for recurring/lifetime obligations (be my mentor forever); ask them to read one blog post. If it goes well, they'll gladly do more.

- ## Heuristic 4 — Make it easy to say no
	- The worst outcome isn't a *no* — it's a **pressured, begrudging yes**.
	- If you get a no: thank them for their time and move on. Guilt-tripping or pestering poisons the relationship while giving you "the false glow of a hard-won victory."
	- "A person who helps you with gritted teeth is one who will never help you again" — and the coerced help is half-hearted anyway. **Help freely given is effortless** (like holding a door open) and is the foundation of a real two-way relationship.

- ## The Hard Constraint
	- **Never lie.** Every ask is attached to *you*. If the reader gets even "a whiff of something off," no request — however small, specific, low-friction, and bounded — gets a yes.

- ## Takeaways
	- Credibility ranking to internalize: **proof of work > personal connection > institutional prestige.**
	- Treat attention as *lent currency*; brevity and relevance-to-them are how you respect it.
	- Optimize for the relationship, not the single yes — freely-given help compounds; coerced help ends the relationship.
