- **Source**: Peter J. Denning, "The Profession of IT: Listening," *Communications of the ACM*, June 2026, Vol. 69 No. 6, pp. 30–33. DOI:10.1145/3808690.
- **One-liner**: Listening is not decoding messages — it's *interpreting the world*. Good coordination, attractive offers, and great customer service all depend on "deep listening": attuning to how the *other* person interprets, not just what they said.

- ## Core Idea: Listening = Interpretation, not Decoding
	- The common **message-exchange model** (sender encodes → adds redundancy → receiver decodes despite noise) equates listening with *accurate decoding*. Works for machines, **fails for humans**.
	- Humans are living organisms constantly interacting; each interaction provokes a **body reaction unique to our history** — that reaction is an *interpretation*. Brains update interpretations day and night; even ancestry shapes them.
	- **No guarantee two parties share the same interpretation** of what's being said → conversations routinely produce different "listenings."
	- Two motivating breakdowns:
		- Spouse: "Tomorrow is trash day" (intended as a request) → other hears mere *information* → no action → both unhappy.
		- Engineer delivers software: "It's what you asked for" / customer: "But it's not what I wanted!" → different listenings of the *requirements*.

- ## Three Dimensions of Interpretation
	- **Language** — what was said, in the context of all that was said before it.
	- **Body** — our physical reactions when speaking and listening.
	- **Mood** — degree of openness toward future possibilities. *The most underappreciated dimension.*
		- Moods = background bodily dispositions toward the future. Examples: **joy** (good things coming), **anxiety** (danger lurks), **resignation** (no new possibilities).
		- The grumpy spouse wasn't open to *any* new request — same words, different mood → different listening.

- ## Coordination & the Conversation for Action (CFA)
	- **Coordination** = getting independent parties to work effectively toward a common goal. Coordinated well ⇒ communicated well.
	- **CFA** — simplest 2-party coordination model. Goal stated as a **Condition of Satisfaction (COS)**, usually with a deadline. Four speech acts / commitments:
		- 1. **A requests** B to produce a COS
		- 2. **B promises** to do it
		- 3. **B declares** delivery
		- 4. **A declares** satisfaction
	- In business the CFA is the **"workflow loop"**; *"complete the loops"* is a productivity motto.
	- **Coordination breakdowns = CFA failures** (a missing/unrecognized speech act). Trash example: A never made a clear request w/ clear COS; B never *listened* a request; both misfired. We're usually **unaware of the CFAs we're in**, so we can't see what broke.
	- Two ways to handle breakdowns:
		- **CFA as literal flowchart** → micromanage the loop → breeds *resentment*.
		- **CFA as a map of commitments** → intervene only to generate the *missing* commitment → breeds *gratitude* ("stuck conversation is moving again"). ← preferred.

- ## Deep Listening (Listening for the Listening)
	- The **offer** (a conditional promise) is a key CFA: performer makes an offer, customer accepts, loop proceeds.
	- **Bedrock of a good offer: it's attractive** — and it's attractive only if it addresses a **concern of the customer**. So you must *listen for concerns*, especially the **unexpressed** ones.
	- **Deep listening = "listening to the listening"** — a form of *empathy*: seeing things the way the other person sees them → *attunement*.
	- **Orienting concerns** = the deep values that guide a person's life and give meaning to their actions. Can't listen well without becoming aware of these.
	- **Asking directly "what are you concerned about?" is unreliable** (people express concerns poorly). Instead:
		- Get them to **tell stories** about themselves, their histories, communities → stories disclose deeper concerns.
		- Listen for their **breakdowns** (events/conditions blocking their goals).
		- Listen for their **moods** — and the assessments/standards behind them; ask clarifying questions.
		- Listen for what makes them **anxious** — anxiety signals they *care* (fear of losing something).
		- Listen for **differences** between how you see things vs. how they do. Don't confuse *your* concern with *theirs*.
		- Adopt a **mood of curiosity**: "What does their world look like?"
	- **Common sin: letting your concerns trump theirs** — jumping in with solutions, assuming your preconceived idea interests them without verifying.

- ## The Dreyfus Skill Hierarchy Applied to Listening
	- Progress by practicing at your current level + coaching from someone more advanced.
	| Level | Name | What you can do |
	|---|---|---|
	| 1 | Beginner | Hear/understand words & sentences. Use the **active listening protocol** — repeat back what you heard; other party verifies "you got it." |
	| 2 | Advanced beginner | Use experience with the person to grasp what they want even if unsaid. Identify **speech acts** & the commitments in them; begin inferring concerns; recognize common **moods**. |
	| 3 | Competent | Impulse to **learn their concerns before stating yours** (Covey: "seek first to understand, then to be understood"). Auto-adjust to sensed moods. |
	| 4 | Proficient | Listen for **unstated concerns** via their stories/history; regularly elicit **"You see me!"**; can **shift moods** to make listeners receptive to offers. |
	| 5 | Expert | Listen for **paradigms & belief systems**; surface people's hidden background assumptions blocking their goals; a good **coach**; orchestrate moods (e.g., team spirit). |
	- **Levels 1–2 are procedural** (protocols/checklists; L2 "caches" familiar situations).
	- **Level 3+** = real *embodiment* — you "know" what's being asked without running the checklist; you see the social world as a **space of many conversations & possibilities**.
	- **Level 5 caution**: pointing out someone's paradigm gets "that's obvious" → defensiveness if challenged. Coaching = helping them become aware of conditioned tendencies and replace them with better practices.

- ## Practice
	- Denning's exercise for innovation-leadership students: **have 10 conversations/week** whose purpose is *not to pitch* but to **listen and learn** what others care about; keep notes to reconstruct the commitments & concerns.
	- Hard at first; after ~5–6 weeks students call these the **highlights of their days**. Learning to listen well makes it far easier to **craft attractive offers**.

- ## Why It Matters (takeaways for work)
	- Requirements/spec failures are often **listening breakdowns**, not documentation failures — the engineer & customer *thought* they agreed on COS until delivery.
	- To make anything land (a proposal, product, request), **map the CFA**: is there a clear request, a real promise (not a vague one), an explicit COS + deadline, and a declaration of satisfaction? Fix the *missing commitment*, don't micromanage the loop.
	- **Sell/serve by listening for concerns**, especially unspoken ones — mine stories, breakdowns, anxieties, and moods rather than asking "what do you want?"
	- Treat listening as a **trainable skill** (Dreyfus L1→L5) with deliberate practice + coaching, not a fixed trait.
